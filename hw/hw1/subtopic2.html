Anti-Virus Software

Summary How They Work What They Defend Against What to Look For


As mentioned before, an anti-virus software on your device will help protect it from various attacks from malware. Malware is any form of software designed to cause harm to another user. Malware comes in many forms. Below is a brief description of some of the most common types of malware. Simply click on one to view the description.

Viruses

This is a program that infects your computer or smartphone without your knowledge and disrupts its normal operation. For a more complete definition, see: http://www.dictionary.com/browse/computer-virus

Worms

This is a program that like a virus will infect your device. A worm however will replicate and travel across your network infecting connected devices. For a more complete definition, see: http://www.tech-faq.com/computer-worm.html

Trojans

Also known as a Trojan horse is a program that is disguised as something safe that when clicked on or accessed maliciously attacks your devices. For a more complete definition, see: https://searchsecurity.techtarget.com/definition/Trojan-horse

Ransomware

This is malicious software that once it has infected your system requires you to pay a ransom to regain access to your device or files. It will do this by either locking the user out from accessing their device or encrypting the files and requiring the user to pay the ransom to get their information decrypted. For a more complete definition, see: https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/us/security/definition/Ransomware

Spyware

Spyware is software that is covertly placed on a user’s device that will record and transfer information regarding the user’s browsing history, emails, and identity back to the source of the malicious software. For a more complete definition, see: https://techterms.com/definition/spyware

Rootkits

Rootkits are probably one of the most dangerous types of malware that exists today. This is a malicious program that will infect a user’s device without their knowledge and provide complete access and control of that device to whomever or whatever is at the source of the rootkit. For a more complete definition, see: https://techterms.com/definition/rootkit
